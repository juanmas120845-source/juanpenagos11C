The Gender Pay Gap in Colombia: A Statistical Analysis of Wage Differences Between Men and Women in the Formal Labor Market

Global Economics · Grade 11 · Term 3, 2025–2026 · Teacher: Nicolás López Cuéllar

Introduction

Wage inequality between men and women remains one of the most studied topics in labor economics. In Colombia, despite advances in gender equality legislation, evidence suggests a persistent gap between average wages earned by men and women in formal employment. According to the Departamento Administrativo Nacional de Estadística (DANE, 2023), women in Colombia earn approximately 12–14% less per hour than men performing equivalent formal-sector jobs. This figure aligns with broader Latin American trends documented by the International Labour Organization (ILO, 2022), which found an average regional gender pay gap of around 17%, even after controlling for education and occupation. Understanding whether this observed difference is statistically significant is central to designing effective public policy (Bernal & Peña, 2020).

Research Question

Is the average monthly formal wage of women in Colombia statistically lower than that of men, at a significance level of α = 0.05?

Methodology

Hypotheses

  • H₀: μwomen ≥ μmen — women's mean wage is at least equal to men's
  • H₁: μwomen < μmen — women's mean wage is strictly lower

Test Direction and Significance Level

The test is left-tailed, since H₁ states that women earn less. The significance level is α = 0.05, standard in social science research (Bernal & Peña, 2020).

Data and Test Statistic

Using DANE's 2022 Gran Encuesta Integrada de Hogares (GEIH) sample data (DANE, 2023), a representative subsample was drawn: n = 400 men with x̄ₘₑₙ = COP 1,850,000 (σ = 420,000) and n = 400 women with x̄ᵥₒₘₑₙ = COP 1,620,000 (σ = 380,000). The male group serves as benchmark.

SE = √(420000²/400 + 380000²/400) ≈ 28,178
z = (1,620,000 − 1,850,000) / 28,178 ≈ −8.16

Results

ParameterValue
Test statistic z−8.16
p-value (left-tailed)< 0.0001
Critical value z₀.₀₅−1.645
Significance level α0.05
DecisionReject H₀

Since z = −8.16 is far below the critical value of −1.645 and p < 0.0001, we reject the null hypothesis. There is overwhelming statistical evidence that women's mean formal wages are significantly lower than men's.

Discussion

The result is consistent with DANE's official statistics showing a 12–14% wage gap in Colombian formal employment (DANE, 2023). The ILO (2022) attributes this gap to occupational segregation, underrepresentation of women in high-wage sectors, and implicit discrimination in salary negotiation. Bernal and Peña (2020) note that even after controlling for education and job type, an unexplained residual gap of approximately 7–9% persists in Colombia, suggesting structural labor market discrimination.

Statistical Errors

  • Type I Error (α = 0.05): A 5% probability of incorrectly concluding women earn less when true means are equal. The negligible p-value makes this highly unlikely here. In practice, this error could lead to implementing unnecessary gender-wage-gap policies.
  • Type II Error (β): The probability of failing to reject H₀ when women do in fact earn less. Given |z| = 8.16, the test has extremely high power, making a Type II Error virtually impossible with these sample sizes.

Conclusion

The statistical evidence strongly supports H₁: the average formal wage of women in Colombia is significantly lower than that of men (z = −8.16, p < 0.0001, α = 0.05). This finding aligns with national and international data on the gender pay gap (DANE, 2023; ILO, 2022) and reinforces the need for evidence-based wage equity policies in the Colombian labor market (Bernal & Peña, 2020). Future research should apply regression-based decomposition to separate the explained and unexplained components of the gap.
